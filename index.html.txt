      <h2>What is Phishing & Social Engineering?</h2>
      <p>
        Phishing and social engineering are techniques attackers use to trick people into
        giving up passwords, money, or sensitive information. Instead of hacking computers,
        they manipulate human trust.
      </p>
    </section>

    <section id="tips">
      <h2>Best Practices to Stay Safe</h2>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Check the sender:</strong> Look closely at the email address or phone number.</li>
        <li><strong>Hover over links:</strong> Make sure the URL matches the real site.</li>
        <li><strong>Watch for urgency:</strong> “Act now or else” is a classic red flag.</li>
        <li><strong>Never share passwords:</strong> Legitimate companies will not ask for them.</li>
        <li><strong>Verify out-of-band:</strong> If unsure, contact the person using a known method.</li>
      </ul>
    </section>

    <section id="examples">
      <h2>Can You Spot the Red Flags?</h2>

      <article class="example safe">
        <h3>Example 1 – Likely Safe</h3>
        <p>
          Your bank sends a monthly statement notification from the same address you always see.
          The email uses your full name and does not ask you to click a link to “verify your account.”
        </p>
        <p><strong>Why it seems safe:</strong> Expected, consistent sender, no pressure, no request for sensitive data.</p>
      </article>

      <article class="example risky">
        <h3>Example 2 – Suspicious</h3>
        <p>
          “URGENT: Your account will be closed in 24 hours! Click here to confirm your password.”
          The sender address is slightly misspelled and the link goes to <code>http://secure-login-bank-support.com</code>.
        </p>
        <p><strong>Red flags:</strong> Urgency, password request, weird URL, spelling issues.</p>
      </article>
    </section>
